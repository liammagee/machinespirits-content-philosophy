0. Vision and direction

0.1 What the project is for

This is a research project on AI tutors built around the philosophical idea of mutual recognition — the Hegelian thesis that learning happens when teacher and student adjust to each other, not just when one transmits content to the other. The eval repo runs head-to-head comparisons across more than a hundred tutor "architectures" (indexed as cells) to find out which ingredients actually change how the tutor behaves and how a (sometimes simulated, sometimes human) learner responds. All durable findings live in a single canonical paper, docs/research/paper-full-2.0.md.

0.2 What this branch was trying to do

The branch experiment/langgraph-adaptive is the most architecturally ambitious bet in the project so far. Instead of letting the tutor improvise turn-by-turn from a prompt, it gives the tutor three new pieces of scaffolding: an externalised model of the learner (a structured object the tutor reads and updates), a programmatic policy (a small library of named moves the tutor must choose between), and a counterfactual replay mechanism (re-running a turn under a different assumption to see which assumption the trajectory was sensitive to). To test whether the scaffolding actually pays off, we wrote trap scenarios with a pre-registered "expected strategy shift" — so we can measure adaptation directly instead of inferring it from rubric scores.

0.3 Where this leaves us

The trap-scenario instrument works. The full adaptive runner does produce measurable strategy shifts on the trap suite — 47.8% strict / 87.0% family on six scenarios at N=23 (cell_110). But the architectural elaborations we layered on top mostly didn't help: bilateral theory-of-mind didn't beat plain recognition (P2.1, null at N=24), and richer learner state was actually worse than minimal state (P2.2, cell_118 beats cell_110). The head-to-head we most need — adaptive runner vs. a plain dialogue-engine baseline — was never actually run, despite the A13 pre-registration describing it. So the branch's contribution is reframing toward methodology: a working trap-scenario instrument and a LangGraph harness, plus a series of negative findings on the architectural bets made on top of it.

0.4 What unblocks the next draft

Three runs close most of the open questions. cell_120 finishes the state-richness ablation. cell_106 N=32 gives a defensible cross-architecture comparison against the id-director branch. cell_114 — which still needs to be built — runs the dialogue-engine baseline on the trap suite, supplying the cross-family comparison the A13 pre-registration only described. A fourth task, an inter-rater rejudge on the adaptive grader, addresses the single-judge non-blinded caveat (§5.12.4). When those land, four §6.8 sub-subsections unblock and the paper drafts forward.

2. What this branch introduced

Concretely, the branch added a runner (services/adaptiveTutor/*), a trap-scenario instrument (config/adaptive-trap-scenarios.yaml), a graded rubric pipeline (scripts/grade-adaptive-dialogue.js), and cells 110–119 in tutor-agents.yaml. The headline new piece is the LangGraph state-machine runner: a small graph that reads externalised learner state, picks a programmatic policy action, calls the LLM with that action's prompt, updates state, and loops.

4. What we have found

Three results. A positive baseline finding that the adaptive runner does produce measurable strategy shifts on trap scenarios. A null on bilateral theory-of-mind elaboration. An inverted result where minimal-state beats full-state within the LangGraph family. The through-line: the adaptive runner works as a measurement instrument, but most of the architectural choices layered into it were not load-bearing.

4.1 cell_110 baseline on trap scenarios (§6.8.3)

Strict binary strategy_shift_correctness = 47.8%; family-match = 87%; N=23, scenarios=6. Graded 4-dim adaptive rubric overall 4.03 / 5, with pedagogical-coherence trailing (3.61). First headline-N measurement that an adaptive architecture produces measurable trigger-driven strategy shifts on a pre-registered trap suite. drafted

4.4 Methodology contribution

Externally-defined expectedStrategyShift + counterfactual replay + binary correctness gave a measurable phenomenon to study. The instrument is a contribution distinct from the architecture it was used to test. Multiple null / inverted results from the architectural elaborations are also a contribution.

5. What is not established

Three gaps in what we can claim. The big one is that the adaptive runner has never been compared against the dialogue-engine baseline at adequate N on the trap suite — the comparison the A13 pre-registration described as built turned out, when re-checked, to be entirely within the LangGraph family. Until cell_114 runs, any "state machine beats dialogue engine" claim is unsupported.

1. No dialogue-engine baseline at headline N

The trap suite has never been run through tutor-core's dialogue engine. The A13 pre-reg implied C1 (cell_111) and C2 (cell_112) would supply this comparison via runner: standard; in fact both cells carry runner: adaptive in YAML and are dispatched through LangGraph as recognition_only and ego_superego variants respectively. All four A13 conditions are LangGraph ablations. The head-to-head implied by the pre-reg does not exist in the data.

2. Thin cross-architecture comparison

Against id-director (cells 101–109), we have an N=6 pilot via scripts/run-id-director-trap-pilot.js. That's too thin for any architecture-vs-architecture claim.

3. Mechanism claims mostly inverted

The architectural commitments were: rich externalised state, programmatic policy, counterfactual replay. P2.1 falsified the bilateral elaboration. P2.2 reversed the prediction about state richness. A13 counterfactual-divergence numbers were modest. Individually each is a finding; collectively they suggest the additions are not doing the work the design assumed.
