Where I'd push back is on the determinism. The essay rides a chain — mimesis → selective alignment → virtue display → capital concentration → new fascism — and each link is plausible in isolation but the whole chain has the smoothness of a chiasm pretending to be an argument. "The deeper the friendship, the greater the betrayal" is rhetorically beautiful and analytically slippery; it's true of any institutional care relationship (doctors, teachers, therapists all operate inside structures whose interests can diverge from the cared-for) without those relationships collapsing into fascism. The essay needs to do more work to show why this case is structurally different rather than just intensified.

I'm also unconvinced "fascism" is the right load-bearing term. Fascism has specific historical content — mass mobilization, ethnonationalism, militarism, the aestheticization of politics. What the essay actually describes is something closer to a soft technofeudalism or atomized influence-capture: not jackboots but personalized persuasion at scale. Calling that fascism gives the argument rhetorical voltage but blurs the diagnosis. The Musk reference does some gesturing here, but the strident-leader figure is exactly what the essay says is being superseded, so it can't also be evidence of continuity.

The immanent-critique framing is doing a lot of work and I'm not sure it earns it. The essay rules out external regulatory critique on the grounds that it presumes a normative position outside the technology — but then proceeds to occupy a normative position outside the technology (Marxist, broadly Frankfurt-school).
