Philosophy of Right

G.W.F. Hegel

1821

Philosophy of Right

The Philosophy of Right presents Hegel’s mature political and ethical philosophy, developing the concept of freedom through its objective manifestations.

Abstract Right

Property

  • The immediate embodiment of freedom
  • Taking possession, use, and alienation
  • Recognition of personality through property

Contract

  • Mutual recognition of property rights
  • The common will between persons
  • The possibility of wrong and crime

Wrong and Crime

  • Unintentional wrong
  • Deception and fraud
  • Crime as the negation of right
  • Punishment as the restoration of right

Morality

Purpose and Responsibility

  • The subjective will and intention
  • Moral responsibility for consequences
  • The right of knowledge

Intention and Welfare

  • The particular purposes of action
  • Individual welfare and happiness
  • The collision of duties

Good and Conscience

  • The unity of the universal and particular will
  • Conscience as subjective certainty
  • The transition to ethical life

Ethical Life (Sittlichkeit)

The Family

  • Marriage as ethical love
  • Family property and capital
  • Education of children
  • Dissolution of the family

Civil Society

  • The system of needs and labor
  • The administration of justice
  • Police and corporation
  • The mediation of particular and universal

The State

  • Constitutional law
  • The rational structure of the state
  • The separation of powers
  • International relations
  • World history as world court

Key Concepts

  1. Freedom: The essence of will and the foundation of right
  2. Recognition: Mutual acknowledgment necessary for freedom
  3. Ethical Life: The unity of subjective morality and objective institutions
  4. Dialectical Development: From abstract to concrete determinations of freedom

Contemporary Relevance

Hegel’s Philosophy of Right addresses: - The relationship between individual and community - The rational basis of political institutions - The role of civil society in modern life - The nature of ethical obligations
