Science of Logic: Doctrine of Being

G.W.F. Hegel

1812

Science of Logic: Doctrine of Being

The Doctrine of Being constitutes the first major division of Hegel’s Science of Logic, beginning with the most abstract and immediate categories.

Pure Being and Nothing

Being

  • The most abstract and immediate thought
  • Pure being without any determination
  • Completely empty and indeterminate

Nothing

  • The absence of all determination
  • Pure emptiness and void
  • Identical to pure being in its abstraction

Becoming

  • The unity and truth of being and nothing
  • The first concrete thought
  • Contains both coming-to-be and ceasing-to-be

Determinate Being (Dasein)

Quality

  • Being with a determinateness
  • Something and Other
  • The dialectic of finitude and infinity

Quantity

  • Being indifferent to determinateness
  • Continuous and discrete magnitude
  • Number as the unity of quantity

Measure

  • The unity of quality and quantity
  • The measureless and the emergence of essence
  • Transition to the Doctrine of Essence

Key Insights

  1. Beginning of Philosophy: Logic must begin with pure being as the most abstract category
  2. Dialectical Movement: Each category generates its opposite and their synthesis
  3. Immanent Development: The categories develop from within, not through external reflection
  4. Systematic Necessity: Each transition is logically necessary, not arbitrary

Significance for the System

The Doctrine of Being establishes: - The fundamental method of dialectical logic - The impossibility of immediate knowledge - The dynamic nature of all categories - The foundation for more concrete logical determinations
