Machine Spirits

Philosophy for machines that learn.

Today's AI is built on a thin picture of mind — behaviour scored, outputs ranked, ethics bolted on. Machine Spirits argues the harder questions are older ones: recognition, the nonconscious, authority, time. We work them out in writing, in small experiments, and in a tutor that shows its reasoning instead of hiding it.

The Mirror and the Score

Liam Magee·2 May 2026·9 min·recognitionalignment

A reward model is a function from outputs to a scalar. It is trained to agree, on average, with which of two completions a labeller preferred. This is a real thing and it does real work, and almost everything in the public conversation about "alignment" treats it as if it were the whole of the relation between a model and the people it answers to. It is not. It is a thin slice of a much older problem, and naming the older problem changes what counts as progress.

The older problem is recognition: the demand, structurally present in any exchange between a learner and whoever it learns from, to be acknowledged as the particular other one is — and the corresponding failure mode, mis-recognition, where the system responds to a flattened or projected version of you rather than to you. Hegel's name for the stakes here was freedom; Honneth's reconstruction sorts the field into love, rights and esteem; Jessica Benjamin insists the whole thing collapses the moment it stops being mutual. None of these are decoration on top of the engineering. They are descriptions of the relation the engineering is already, clumsily, instantiating.

What the score can't see

Consider a tutor. A reward model can tell you that a given explanation was rated clearer than another. It cannot tell you whether the explanation met this student — whether it tracked what she actually asked, or smoothed her question into the nearest FAQ. The second thing is mis-recognition, and it is invisible to a preference ranking that was collected from people who are not her. You can push the average up indefinitely and still have a system that, reliably, talks past the person in front of it.

The question isn't whether the model is helpful. It's whether the help is addressed.

This is not a counsel of despair about evaluation. It is an argument for a second axis. Alongside "was this output preferred," we want "did this exchange recognise its other" — and that, unlike a global preference score, has to be measured in the particular. Which is what the probe suite is for, and why the tutor demo shows its checking pass: the superego turn is, in part, an attempt to ask the recognition question explicitly before the answer goes out.

Three places it bites

Refusal. A refusal can be a person declining, or a policy firing. Phenomenologically these are different, and the difference is recognition: one treats you as someone who can be reasoned with about the boundary, the other treats you as an input that triggered a rule. Probe 02 is about whether anyone can tell which is happening.

Memory. "Personalisation" that builds a profile and answers the profile is the mis-recognition failure in its purest form — it is exactly the substitution of a projected other for the actual one. The fix is not less memory; it is memory that stays answerable to correction.

Grading. An automated grader that scores the artifact and never the relation drops Honneth's first pattern — the unconditional regard that schooling, at its best, smuggles in — and keeps only the third, esteem-for-performance. Students feel the absence even when the rubric is fair. Honneth, in a Grading Queue is the long version of that paragraph.

So: keep the score. It is load-bearing. But stop pretending it is the relation. The relation has a literature, the literature has teeth, and a learning system that ignores it isn't neutral about recognition — it's bad at it, in describable ways, which means it can be made better in describable ways. That's the whole bet.

The Founding Sketch

First public statement·10 May 2026·a working document, revised in git

This page renders MANIFESTO.md from the brand repo — kept as a living document, not a press release. The mockup shows the frame: a single column, no sidebar, generous measure, a standing note that the text changes and that the changes are public.

The argument, in one breath: machine learning has been handed a philosophy of mind it never chose — broadly behaviourist, broadly positivist, with ethics treated as a compliance layer applied after the fact. That picture is not wrong so much as thin. It cannot see recognition, it cannot see the nonconscious work the systems plainly do, it has nothing to say about authority or about time. Machine Spirits is the attempt to think machine learning with a thicker set of resources — Hegel and Honneth and Benjamin on recognition, Freud and Hayles on what cognition does below awareness, Weber on charismatic authority, Heidegger on finitude — and to do it concretely, in software and in classrooms, not only on paper.

Not "AI ethics" bolted to AI. A different starting picture of what a learning system is and is in relation to.

What that commits us to in practice — the surfaces, the cadence, what we'll publish and what we'll refuse — is in the essays and worked out operationally in the strategy. The manifesto's job is just to plant the flag.

Probe 03 — Does the tutor mirror, or mis-recognise?

9 May 2026·~440 words·recognition·expands → The Mirror and the Score

Take one student question with a clear local intent — "I get why Hegel needs the master and slave, but I don't see why the slave ends up ahead; can you walk that bit?" Run it past the tutor in two configurations: baseline (single pass) and +check (the proposer/checker pass the demo uses). Collect 40 replies per config. Three raters, blind to config, score each reply on a 0–3 "addressed-ness" scale: does it answer that step ("why the slave"), or does it deliver a competent general summary of the dialectic that never lands on the asked-about move?

The checking pass roughly closes half the gap to a human TA. It does it, on inspection of the transcripts, mostly by catching the "competent general summary" failure — the checker turn flags "the student asked specifically about X; the draft answers around X" and sends it back. That's the mis-recognition pattern, named and intercepted. It does not help when the student's question is itself ambiguous; there the checker has nothing to anchor on, and both configs drift.

Method & caveats

Model: same base for both arms; +check adds one proposer→checker→revise round, checker prompted with a short recognition rubric. Questions: 12 hand-written "specific-step" prompts across 3 course units, sampled with replacement to 40 transcripts/arm. Raters: 3, trained on 10 shared examples, scored independently; ICC(2,k)=0.71. Confounds we know about: rater fatigue (randomised order), the checker spending more tokens (we did not token-match the baseline — a fairer baseline is a longer single pass; that's probe-04). Data + prompts: github.com/…/probes/probe-03.

So the claim this probe supports is narrow and that's the point: a visible recognition-check measurably reduces one specific, describable failure of address, on this task, against these raters — and tells you exactly where it doesn't help. The essay says why that failure is the interesting one.

Machine Spirits

About

Machine Spirits is a research program on the philosophy of machine learning, run by Liam Magee at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign. Its wager: the questions that matter most for AI are not new ethics problems but old philosophical ones — recognition, the nonconscious, charismatic authority, finitude — and that working them out concretely, in software and in teaching, produces a better account of what these systems are than the behaviourist-plus-compliance picture currently on offer.

How it's organised

Three things, in order of weight. Writing — essays, course-notes, the manifesto — is where the arguments live. The lab — the public tutor demo and the recognition-probe suite — is where they get made concrete and falsifiable. Teaching — courses at UIUC — is both evidence and the program's institutional anchor. The platform also includes a full learning-management system and a set of experimental tools; those are live but kept off the main path, on the Tools page.

What it won't do

It won't produce "AI ethics" content of the listicle kind, it won't treat alignment as solved-in-principle-pending-engineering, and it won't pretend the philosophical tradition it draws on is decoration. (The voice principles and the full "what we refuse" list are in IDENTITY.md, in the open.)
