Research Paper

The Dialectics of the Circle: Kandinsky's Geometric Epistemology and Contemporary Cognition

Dr. Helena Kreis March 15, 2025 24 min read

This paper investigates how Kandinsky's theoretical framework of geometric forms as carriers of spiritual content finds unexpected resonance in contemporary cognitive science, particularly in research on embodied cognition and the phenomenology of abstract perception.

Introduction

Wassily Kandinsky's theoretical writings, particularly "Point and Line to Plane" (1926), have long been regarded as foundational texts in the pedagogy of abstract art. Yet their implications extend far beyond the studio or the art history seminar. In proposing that geometric forms possess inherent expressive qualities—that a circle "speaks" differently than a triangle, that a horizontal line evokes calm while a diagonal suggests dynamism—Kandinsky was making claims about the nature of human perception and cognition that anticipate developments in cognitive science by nearly a century.

"The circle is the synthesis of the greatest oppositions. It combines the concentric and the eccentric in a single form, and in equilibrium."
— Wassily Kandinsky

Geometric Forms as Cognitive Primitives

Recent research in visual cognition has demonstrated that the human visual system processes geometric primitives—circles, triangles, rectangles—as fundamental units of perception. These forms are recognized pre-attentively, suggesting they occupy a privileged position in our cognitive architecture. Kandinsky's intuition that such forms carry inherent meaning finds support in studies showing consistent cross-cultural associations between geometric shapes and emotional or conceptual content.

The Embodied Circle

For Kandinsky, the circle held special significance. He described it as "the most modest form, but asserts itself unconditionally" and "a precise but inexhaustible variable." Contemporary embodied cognition research suggests why: the circle, unlike the triangle or square, has no beginning or end, no corners that anchor attention. It invites continuous, cyclical scanning—a visual rhythm that may correspond to fundamental patterns in human movement and respiration.
