## What is Phenomenology? Why should we care?

Everyday understanding: phenomenon - a thing, an object, an event, a spectacle. Colloquial: "phenomenonal" - spectacular, amazing, huge etc.

*phainomenai* (Ancient Greek): to appear, to make present

## Key moments

 - Descartes (17th century France / Sweden): how things appear to me (not always how they are). What is the minimal thing of which I can be certain? That I am here thinking these thoughts (*cogito ergo sum*). Perhaps unfairly responsible for mind/body and subjective thought / objective reality distinction.

## Key moments

 - Hegel (early 19th century Germany): Phenomenology of Mind / Spirit (Geist). A study of the movement of mind / spirit towards its ultimate realization (from sense to perception to consciousness to self-consciouness to reason to spirit to absolute spirit). 
 - Husserl (early 20thth century Germany). Return to Descartes (*Cartesian Meditations*). 
 - Heidegger (early/mid 20thth century Germany). The study of what it is to *be*; and specifically, to be the kind of thing that asks about *being*. Contra-Descartes (and Husserl), we are always in a world - *thrown* into it (*Dasein* = "being there"). We cannot study phenomena without being also constituted by it (by language, environment, culture etc). Origins of *existentialism* (Sartre etc). 
 - Merleau-Ponty: reinterprets Husserl, Heidegger with an emphasis on sensory perception, embodiment.

## Relevance for social research 

 - Phenomenology responds to a problem of traditional research:
 - I ask you a question (in a survey / interviews)
 - I follow best-practice - the question has been asked before 
 - Take an example (from Teachers’ Acceptance of AI in Education (TAAI) instrument by Guo, Shi, & Zhai):
   - "I am willing to use AI tools to assess students." - 5 point Likert scale (Strongly disagree = 1 … Strongly agree = 5)
   - Validated by expert review
   - All good?


---

## "I am willing to use AI tools to assess students."

 - Am I? It depends...
   - Sometimes I'm impatient - Perhaps I use AI to check I'm not being too hasty?
   - Perhaps I am angry at my institution, pushing me to use AI - I deliberately refuse it (but I might use it in a different institution)
   - Which tools? It could depend...
   - Which student? It could depend...

So what is really measured? 

Even qualitative research doesn't *solve* this issue. While I am in my "official" pose as a teacher I might be concerned about perception ("social desirability bias").

 - "I don't condone use of AI by students, so I wouldn't use it myself either."
 - "AI is fantastic! Our school is right behind it, and so am I! Of course we need to be thoughtful about how its used..."

## Is the truth out there? How do we get it?

Does phenomenonology solve these problems? Of course not, but it can aim - both in interview technique and data analysis - to capture "how things (actually) appear to the participant". Get as close as possible to the *experience* of the partipant - not just what they think. An example:

 - recall a (recent) incident of using AI for assessment (or when you refused to use it)
 - what led up to that incident?
 - what went through your mind as you prompted the AI for assessment? 
 - how did you feel about the results? 
 - reflect on any emotions / perceptions. Anxiety? Guilt? Pleasure? If so, why?

## Limitations

 - Tensions with thematic analysis (can experience be 'cut up'?)
 - Memory is notoriously unreliable
 - Social desirability bias
 - Requires a 'leap of faith' - what constitutes proper, valid, reliable knowledge?
