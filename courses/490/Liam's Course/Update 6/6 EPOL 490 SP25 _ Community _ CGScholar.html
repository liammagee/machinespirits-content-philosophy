EPOL 490 SP25’s Updates

“The Problem(s) of Alignment”

This week we turn from the comparatively benign world of learning and training to enter that of AI in the wild – and the many problems that emerge with aligning or counter-aligning behavior.

The focus for the week is the now-classic "Stochastic Parrots" paper by Emily Bender, Timnit Gebru, Angelina McMillan-Major and Margaret Mitchell. We'll spend some time talking this paper through, as it has influenced what has widely come to be known as "Critical AI" (and launched a journal of the same name).

I have contributed to two of the papers this week. The first, on truth, is partly a response to the Stochastic Parrots paper, and other conversations in relation to AI and hallucination. Working in part from Foucault's discussion of *parrhesia*, discussed in a previous week, in that paper we sought to think about the different ways truth itself is understood, and how this might reflect upon our judgments of AI. In particular I want to unpack the example of "two plus two equals".

In the second paper, we look at the question of alignment from an historical standpoint. We argue there that alignment is closely connected to quite fundamental questions of how people use language normatively. Alignment in other words relates to processes of control via language: not only in the obvious cases of censorship, but also in the intricate ways in which language marks and reproduces social power. We are perhaps today on the verge of seeing AI that can employ subtle linguistic cues to align us - a kind of counter-alignment or manipulation, as interesting case of Claude (Denison et al. 2024) shows.

If you're stretched for time, I'd read the articles in that order (Bender et al, Munn et al, Hristova et al, Denison et al). We'll spend most of the time in class with the Bender et al reading.

For those looking for further reading in Critical AI, I've included several other key texts, including a reading list that will be especially useful for those of you also looking to teach Critical AI.

In addition – if you're looking for a question to think about ahead of class – try to think of a single sentence that would articulate all you could ever want from your "dream" aligned AI.

  • What struck me most in this week’s readings was Hristova et al.’s (2024) view of alignment as more than just a technical safeguard. They frame it as a form of social control through language. That idea connected with my own work in education. In assessment, the way we use rubrics and standards already sets the boundaries for what counts as good work. That can be useful, but it can also narrow student voice.
    Stochastic Parrots (Bender et al., 2021) raised a similar concern for me. Just because something sounds fluent doesn’t mean there’s real understanding behind it. I see this in my classroom when multilingual learners can “sound right” but still miss the deeper meaning. AI runs the same risk. It might reward answers that look polished but miss the point. If students start adjusting their language just to match the system’s preferences, we lose the chance for authentic expression.

    Munn et al. (2023) push this even further, showing how truth itself is contextual and socially constructed. What counts as “correct” in one setting may look very different in another. Hristova’s warning about counter-alignment captures the danger here: instead of us shaping AI to our needs, we might end up quietly aligning ourselves to its patterns. For education, that feels risky.

    My dream aligned AI would do the opposite. It would support growth with feedback that feels personal, admit where it falls short, and encourage students to use their own voices instead of copying patterns.

    References

    Bender, E. M., Gebru, T., McMillan-Major, A., & Shmitchell, S. (2021). On the dangers of stochastic parrots: Can language models be too big? Proceedings of the 2021 ACM Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency, 610–623. https://doi.org/10.1145/3442188.3445922

  • As a personal business owner of hand-made goods, this hits home. Just as large language models (LLMs) generate text by predicting the next most probable word in a sequence based on statistical patterns from vast datasets, AI art models create images by assembling and refining patterns derived from existing artwork in their training data. They don’t understand artistic principles or composition the way a human artist does; instead, they recombine learned visual elements in response to a prompt.

    AI art generators, like MidJourney, Stable Diffusion, and DALL·E, are essentially visual stochastic parrots—they don’t create from genuine artistic intuition but rather from learned correlations. They have been trained on enormous datasets of human-created images, identifying recurring styles, textures, compositions, and even artistic conventions. When prompted, they synthesize these patterns into new images that resemble human-made works but do not emerge from independent creative thought.

    The ethical issue arises because these AI models are trained on datasets that often include copyrighted artwork without the explicit consent of the original artists. Unlike human artists, who study, learn, and develop their own unique styles over time, AI can generate artwork in seconds that might closely resemble existing works, sometimes even carrying over distinct features of a particular artist’s style. This leads to concerns that AI-generated images are derivative works built on unauthorized data.

    Some artists argue that this is tantamount to theft, as AI companies are effectively using their work to train models that can then create similar images without attribution or compensation. Others contend that this is no different from human artists being inspired by past works—though the key difference is AI does this at an unprecedented scale and speed, without individual artistic intent.

    The rise of prompt artists—people who craft detailed text prompts to generate AI artwork—adds another layer to the debate. Some argue that there is a genuine skill in mastering prompts to achieve high-quality results, much like directing an artist or curating images. However, many critics counter that typing a well-worded request into an AI tool is fundamentally not the same as traditional artistic craftsmanship, which requires years of skill-building, creativity, and manual effort.

    The controversy intensifies when AI-generated images are sold commercially, often with the prompt artist claiming full authorship. Since the AI itself is doing the heavy lifting, some argue that selling AI-generated art without acknowledging its machine-generated nature is misleading—or even exploitative, especially when it takes opportunities away from traditional artists.

  • I found the Truth Machines journal article particularly interesting because it is pertinent to my work. As Munn et al. (2023) describe, While RLHF knowledge leverages human insight, this is attended by all-too-human subjectivity. van der Lee et al (2021) worry that annotators will engage in satisficing, succumbing to tedium and fatigue and taking shortcuts to arrive at low-quality answers. Although training models for accuracy is a priority in training tasks, so is lowering cost to optimize profits. Often training tasks have time limits, beyond which (depending on the company) taskers don't receive payment or their tasks time out without pay, and others may commandeer the work. While they work, an ever-present timer displays their task's countdown.

    These time limits vary considerably, in my experience from 25 minutes to 150 minutes per task. Within those limits, taskers (writers, reviewers, editors, or auditors) have rubrics with several criteria to score for two responses. They must usually also provide a detailed written justification about which response they prefer overall and why. Hybrid human/AI teams check work at the four levels before the final submissions get fed to the new model, but each step has a timer. Therefore, taskers may not only experience tedium and take shortcuts, but the pay structure penalizes them if they don't.

    For example, a task might involve reading through two five-day travel itineraries, which include directions, tourist sights, recommended restaurants for each meal, and alternative hotels—all requiring high ratings and within a set price. Within 30 minutes, a tasker may need to research dozens of facts in each response, highlighting inaccuracies, and then rate each for clarity, helpfulness, etc. Each response likely contains factual inaccuracies: a hotel that closed, a street name from another city, a restaurant that never existed, etc. The tasker might glance at the timer and see only a few minutes remain, hurry to select a response, and submit the work before the clock runs out. The preferred answer may still contain several unnoticed flagrant falsehoods. The systemic hope is that another tasker further in the process will catch and fix the errors, but everyone operates with time restrictions. Munn et al. (2023) describe this process as an assembly line in a Truth Factory, where the training team's accuracy is subjective, inconsistent, and insufficient.
