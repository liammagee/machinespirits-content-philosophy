EPOL 490 SP25’s Updates

Accelerate Now!

This week we continue with reading and discussion of Bender et al.'s influential Stochastic Parrots paper – in part because it brings up criticisms that remain relevant even with the changing face of AI today.

In addition this week – and as time permits – we broaden still further the scope of our inquiry. One of the objects of Bender et al.'s critique is the implicit assumption that bigger means better: the larger the language model, the closer we reach to Artificial General or Super Intelligence.

We might ask a follow-up question: what does it mean if this goal, or something like it, is realised? What sort of society do we have to look forward to? It is common to imagine a dystopic or a naively utopic future, but the readings I've picked here instead pull from different perspectives to think of – arguably – more nuanced positions. They broadly come under the title of "accelerationism": the idea that this car we are driving into the future needs no breaks, but instead all force applied to the accelerator pedal.

As always, not all of the readings are required. Scan or read what you can. We start with one of the most famous statements of optimism in our technological destiny, from perhaps an unlikely source: Karl Marx. We then examine an important statement of futurist Marinetti, who more than any other captured – in the 1910s – the enthusiasm for near-future. We then – excuse the pun – accelerate toward the recent past, where accelerationism has enjoyed a voluminous, if largely subcultural, life on various heterodox platforms and publications.

It might be asked why this emphasis on what seems a strange philosophical offshoot (sidenote: you'll see references to Deleuze & Guattari, two hugely influential figures of the 70s and 80s). However the core issues are ones that underpin an enormous amount of the discourse on both left and right side of politics around AI today. Peter Thiel, for example, is an influential Silicon Valley investor, with strong ties to Elon Musk and J.D. Vance. Alex Williams and Nick Srnicek, on the other hand, were at one time connected to Jeremy Corbin, leader of the British Labor party for several years. Meanwhile, of the thousands of articles and op ed pieces written by the producers of AI, I've selected a relatively modest and careful article by Anthropic's CEO, Dario Amodei.

As we will conclude next week, how we think about generative AI for education depends in part on how we think about technology in relation to "big picture" social topics: the future of work, the possibility of abundance, the prospects of wealth redistribution, the geopolitical matrix assembling around AI, and the destiny of the human.

Question for the week: You'll notice this week is heavily sprinkled with "manifestos" of different kinds. Why? What is it about technology that inclines people to declare to make things manifest?

  • This week’s emphasis on manifestos made me think about how technology almost demands big declarations. Marx’s fragment on machines, Marinetti’s call for futurism, and Haraway’s cyborg essay all felt to me like attempts to set a course for society rather than describe the nuts and bolts of technology. Reading them together, I started to see manifestos as something that surfaces when a technology is new or uncertain and people feel the need to declare their hopes or warnings.

    In AI, we see the same impulse. Bender et al.’s “Stochastic Parrots” paper (2021) wasn’t called a manifesto, but it read like one: a bold statement that warned the field about scale and bias before those issues spun out of control. Crawford’s Atlas of AI (2021) also functions as a kind of manifesto in its critique of power and extraction. Even Amodei’s Machines of Loving Grace (2024) fits the genre. He doesn’t just describe possible applications, he sketches a future where AI could transform health, governance, and economic development if we steer it responsibly.

    As an educator, I recognize the same pattern in ed-tech. When learning management systems or adaptive testing platforms are introduced, they are often pitched in manifesto-like language: this will revolutionize learning, this will free teachers to do what matters. In practice, the technology usually reinforces existing structures. Cope and Kalantzis (2022) describe cybernetics as an early attempt to model communication and control across humans and machines. One way to read this history is that cybernetics carried the ambition of automating aspects of decision-making, which still echoes in today’s tools that promise to “optimize” learning but often just digitize existing routines.

    So why manifestos? Maybe because technology moves so quickly that people feel the need to declare where it should take us, even if reality never quite matches the vision. For education, that raises a question for me: should we as teachers and program leaders be writing our own manifestos for AI? If the loudest declarations come from industry, we risk letting their vision define how AI enters classrooms. What would it look like if educators staked out our own claims for how AI should support—not replace—learning?

Very possibly. Our technological capacity to automate borders on science fiction. But that vision certainly hasn't manifested itself; we're probably a lot closer to Marx's fears than his dreams right now, and I think the disconnect is that we tend to expect technology to revolutionize by itself. It's easy to write a manifesto about how technology could or should revolutionize our futures, but it's a lot harder to restructure reality to bring that revolution to pass.

Look at my utopia, they write, and we'll figure the rest out later.

Using this theory as a lens, as we become used to AI being integrated into each device and site we use, our acceptance of it being there will increase and the likelihood of use would also increase. The Technology Acceptance Model (Davis, 1986 & 1989) provides further insight into this as users become more versed in AI use from simple companions like Meta AI, ChatGPT, and Grok, they will likely have less panicked thoughts about the uprise of AI as they will see the benefits and harmlessness of it in those senses.

    Despite this, there are downsides to this thought process as well. Simple pathways to acceptance may lead society as a whole to ignore a need for regulations to protect data and copyrighted work. While a manifesto or social media post or professional development may help with the overall acceptance and growth of artificial intelligence systems, it is important to recognize within those manifestos the drawbacks and safety considerations as well. I believe Amodei's article treads this ground well, providing a positive outlook that still recognizes threats and downsides.

It is no surprise that accelerationists today echo some of his ideas, arguing that technology should not just be embraced but pushed to its limits.
    Then there's Marx’s “Fragment on Machines”, which almost reads like a 19th-century version of an AI debate with machines reducing human labor, shifting power, and hinting at a post-capitalist world. The #ACCELERATE Manifesto takes this further, claiming that capitalism itself is slowing technological progress and must be superseded.
    I believe that people feel compelled to write manifestos about technology because it is not neutral. It forces decisions about power, control, and the future. The political situation worldwide is uncertain and tense and when the stakes are high, people do not just debate, they declare.

  • While not a full response to the reading, I had a thought after reading this update and the question of the week. Technology has shared connections with its root techne, a Greek word that connotes skillful making and craft. Manifestos and manifesting are about bringing into reality something from a preceding intent or desire.

    So, the connection can be quite clear: they are both about creation in some common sense. I think there's something a little trickier going on though, as well. It could be argued that the products of technological creation often are often incorporated into networks of practice and use that may have little to do with the intention of the makers or designer. The internet is one example. Phonographs were intended as business tools but became music players.

    On the other side, a manifesto (and whatever products and objects it brings along or inspires) could be seen as wearing a heavier cloak of meanings and perspectives that is harder to shake off. So, to the original question of the week - the desire is to inject a controlling set of values and possibilities into the product of techne.
