EPOL 490 SP25’s Updates

Pavlovian Machines? Supervised, Unsupervised and Reinforcement Learning

This week we turn to ideas of machine learning that borrow more directly from theories of human learning.

As with Week 2, a couple of the papers (Ouyang et al. 2022; Guo et al., 2025) are again quite technical. I'd encourage skimming each, and paying greater attention to the methods of training. Ouyang et al. (2022) for example applies Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback to refine GPT-3 into GPT-3.5 – becoming, by the end of 2022, ChatGPT. I'd suggest reviewing Guo et al.'s discussion of DeepSeek-R1-Zero – a key moment in how *unsupervised* reinforcement learning is now becoming possible. Also worth glancing at is the DeepMind paper on AlphaZero – a seminal moment in unsupervised learning. Finally, on a technical front, the Delua paper describes the general differences between supervised and unsupervised learning.

I've included two psychology papers: one by Skinner, describing "operant behavior", a classic paper of behaviouralism that reminds us – and helps to inspire – supervised learning. Kohler's discussion of Pavlov's dogs belongs to a similar tradition. Dewey's approach to experiential learning seems similar, instead, to unsupervised learning.

Finally, Cope & Kalantzis' history of cybernetics and the early days of AI makes some of these connections more explicit.

Again, there's a lot - and no need to read everything. Particularly on the technical material, feel free to scan and select sections that seem to echo earlier theories of human learning.

A provocation for next week: machine learning borrows from theories of learning devised in the early part of the twentieth century. What has changed in how we view human learning since? And – if those changes are significant – why aren't machines trained upon these updated theories and lessons?

  • What stood out to me this week is how much today’s machine learning still draws on early behaviorist theories like Skinner’s operant conditioning (Skinner, 1963). Reinforcement learning, whether supervised or unsupervised, looks a lot like the old “reward and punishment” cycle. That connection makes sense technically, but in education we have long known the limits of this way of thinking.

    Chomsky showed that behaviorism didn’t fully explain language. Kids often say things they’ve never been taught, which means something else is going on (Chomsky, 1959). That realization pushed educators to look past simple stimulus and response and toward theories that give more weight to experience, context, and social interaction (Dewey, 1986; Vygotsky, 1978).

    In my own work with multilingual learners, this history feels very relevant. Assessment is not only about whether students get the answer right. It is also about how they make sense of tasks, how they use feedback, and what they can do with support inside their zone of proximal development. Reading Ouyang et al. (2022) on reinforcement learning with human feedback made me wonder: if AI keeps relying on behaviorist methods, will it ever really fit classrooms that emphasize constructivist learning?

    Some of the new approaches, like Guo et al.’s (2025) work on unsupervised reinforcement learning, seem to be testing the boundaries. They remind me of Dewey’s idea of experiential learning, where the process matters as much as the outcome. Even so, I worry that AI might still reinforce surface behaviors while missing the social and collaborative side of learning. As Crawford (2021) argues in Atlas of AI, these systems always reflect the historical choices behind their design. That makes me wonder if behaviorist training persists because it is easier to implement technically, even if it doesn’t align with what we know about human learning.

    References

    Chomsky, N. (1959). Review of B. F. Skinner’s Verbal Behavior. Language, 35(1), 26–58.

    Crawford, K. (2021). Atlas of AI: Power, politics, and the planetary costs of artificial intelligence. Yale University Press.

    Dewey, J. (1986). Experience and education. Taylor & Francis Group.

    Guo, D., Yang, D., Zhang, H., Song, J., Zhang, R., Xu, R., … & He, Y. (2025). Deepseek-r1: Incentivizing reasoning capability in LLMs via reinforcement learning. https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12948

    Ouyang, L., Wu, J., Jiang, X., Almeida, D., Wainwright, C., Mishkin, P., … & Lowe, R. (2022). Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, 35, 27730–27744.

    Skinner, B. F. (1963). Operant behavior. American Psychologist, 18(8), 503.

    Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). Mind in society: The development of higher psychological processes. Harvard University Press.

    How Has Our View of Human Learning Changed?
    From Behaviorism to Cognition: Early theories (like those of B.F. Skinner and Pavlov) saw learning as a process of conditioning—reinforcing correct behaviors and minimizing errors. Today, we recognize that learning is not just about input-output reactions but also involves active thinking, problem-solving, and reflection (Piaget, Vygotsky).
    Social and Contextual Learning: We now know that learning happens in social contexts, with people constructing knowledge through interaction (Vygotsky’s Zone of Proximal Development). Unlike machines, humans learn through dialogue, culture, and shared experiences—something AI still struggles with.
    Neuroscience and Brain Plasticity: Advances in neuroscience have shown that learning is dynamic, with brain structures adapting over time (neuroplasticity). Human learning isn’t just statistical pattern recognition; it involves emotions, motivation, and the ability to generalize across experiences in ways that AI does not replicate well.
    If These Changes Are Significant, Why Aren't Machines Trained on Newer Learning Theories?
    Efficiency Over Depth: Machine learning relies on pattern recognition and optimization because these methods scale well for large datasets. Modern theories of human learning often require context, reasoning, and adaptability, which are harder to formalize in algorithms.
    Lack of Common Sense & Abstraction: AI models struggle with understanding concepts the way humans do. While they recognize patterns well, they don’t form deep, interconnected knowledge structures like humans.
    The Challenge of Social Learning: Social and contextual learning (e.g., learning through dialogue and real-world interaction) requires AI to develop a sense of meaning, culture, and personal experiences, which remains a major hurdle in AI research.
    The Black Box Problem: Modern deep learning models operate in a black-box fashion—recognizing patterns but without clear reasoning paths. Applying cognitive science principles, such as explicit reasoning and self-reflection, would require a fundamental shift in AI architecture.
    Current AI is Good Enough for Many Applications: AI today excels at prediction, pattern matching, and reinforcement learning (which are based on older theories). Since these methods already work well in tasks like language processing, image recognition, and automation, there hasn’t been enough pressure to shift toward more complex human-like learning models.
    Could Future AI Learn More Like Humans?
    Yes! Researchers are actively exploring hybrid models that integrate symbolic reasoning, contextual learning, and emotional intelligence into AI.   • The readings this week for me were a refreshing mix of the popular constructivist learning theories in education today, the history of cybernetics, the behavioral old school theories of the past, along with the discovery of how LLMs are trained today which can be defined by the specific data types used (supervised or unsupervised). With regard to the question about the possibility of moving away from behaviorist pedagogies for training LLMs towards a more modern constructivist pedagogy, I think the reason is so obvious, yet not as easy to put into words. Knowing how complex the human system is, so complex, in fact, that humans have been able to create and train these wonderfully complicated LLM machines to imitate so many human cognitive abilities!

    That’s certainly a credit to humankind! But humankind has had to train LLMs to imitate human cognitive functional abilities through the Pavlov/Skinner Behaviorist pedagogy due to the nature of machine-kind so-to-speak. Machines are not complex enough or capable enough to have independent individual experiences in the world to be taught using a social constructivist pedagogy and I don’t see how they ever will be. To strengthen this position, I’ll add a discussion of a contemporary learning theory that’s even newer than Constructivism. And that’s Connectivism.

    Connectivism, an educational theory developed by George Siemens evolved in the early 2000’s and continues through today from observing how most people learn things in modern life—by searching the internet, by being part of a network and by interacting socially with other people. Certainly machines can be trained on the new information being fed into the network and from people, but machines cannot independently make decisions on how they choose to learn, which people they will listen to for information and how to connect/disconnect/reconnect to a network of communication without human assistance. Siemmens (2004) posits that “Connectivism presents a model of learning that acknowledges the tectonic shifts in society where learning is no longer an internal, individualistic activity.” (p. 6). To me what this implies is that learning has become increasingly more about making decisions as to how a person will connect to the outside world. They can now choose what they will learn and how they choose to learn it recursively, by making their own decisions regarding the people and the networks they choose to connect with over and over. Learning in this manner while adapting to shifting world views and continually making decisions/evaluations/re-evaluations about their current connections and future connections is outside of the realm of “machine-kind” capability without enormous assistance from humans.

    Reference:
    Siemens, G. (2004). Elearnspace. Connectivism: A learning theory for the digital age. Elearnspace. org, 14-16.

  • In education, we talk a lot about intrinsic versus extrinsic motivation. Ideally, my students would be driven to learn by curiosity, a passion for the subject, a desire to learn and grow, and so on. A significant part of my job as a teacher is to structure my class in such a way that instills those inspirations in students, creating that intrinsic drive that makes them want to learn. That doesn't always work, of course, and if my classes had a 100% success rate with instilling intrinsic motivation to learn in every teenager who entered them I'm pretty sure I'd qualify for at least one Nobel. Grades tend to be our go-to extrinsic motivators--essays count for 20% of the final grade in my class, and that alone typically ensures at least decent effort on them--and things like extra credit or pizza parties tend to pick up the slack where needed. On the whole, though, I'd like to think that a well-structured, well-taught class manages to provide at least some intrinsic motivation to most students most of the time.

    Machine learning, by its very nature, can't benefit from that intrinsic motivation. If I am training an AI, I can't motivate the AI to learn and grow by nurturing its passions or curiosities because it doesn't have them. That limitation, I think, keeps AI training grounded in earlier learning theories like Behaviorism because they rely on a simple teach-reward model rather than something like Constructivism, which in many ways requires at least some level of intrinsic motivation.

    It is interesting to think, though, about what it would take to instill an intrinsic motivation to learn in an AI. Is there some sort of drive to learn independently that could be programmed into a machine? Would we want that, even if it were possible? For now, I think, a Behaviorist model works well for training AIs for the same reason it doesn't work for teaching our human students: the educator retains a firm grip on what the learner learns and how and when.
