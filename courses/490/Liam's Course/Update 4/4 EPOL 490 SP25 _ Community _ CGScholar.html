How Do We Talk to AI?

How Do We Talk to AI?

This week we move on from the more technically intensive readings to think about the question: how do we talk to machines?

This (no less demanding) question is, on the one hand, the topic of a thousand social media posts promising to teach you everything you need to know about the new (and dubiously named) discipline of "prompt engineering". On the other, it also opens up a more profound space for exploring what it means to talk at all – with machines, with each other, with ourselves.

Accordingly I've selected an array of readings this week that aim to stretch our thinking about "talk", as a performance (Austin 1962), as a defiant act of truth (Foucault 2020), and as user interaction (Weizenbaum 1966, Mollick & Mollick 2023, Magee et al. 2024).

I want first to reflect a little on J.L. Austin's seminal text, "How to Do Things With Words". This book – a series of lectures – instigated attention to the "pragmatics" of language, and inspired, among others, Searle's Chinese room thought experiment. Again I don't suggest reading this end-to-end, but perhaps focus on getting some conceptual understanding of Austin's distinctions between:

- performative / constative utterances
- locutionary / illocutionary / perlocutionary speech acts

Perhaps the question for us is "How to Get AI to Do Things with Words".

Then, with Ethan and Lilach Mollick's paper, we will examine how different prompts can be used to play roles on the pedagogical "stage". I've also assigned a paper I have developed recently with colleagues titled "The Drama Machine". It is long, and I don't suggest you read all of it, but rather focus on the principal idea: that LLMs have internalized vast amounts of information about how people perform with language, and this internalization can simulate quite complex character enactments.

Both papers are in some ways throwbacks to the early days of AI, when psychoanalysis and dramaturgy were more prominent theoretical rubrics. As if on cue, we look at a classic AI paper, Weizenbaum's 1966 presentation of ELIZA: a system that simulated a certain therapeutic approach, to surprising effect given the technical limits of the time.

Finally, for those with theoretical inclinations, I've included Michel Foucault's work on "Discourse and Truth and Parrhesia". Perhaps more of a placeholder for discussions in future weeks, Foucault's analysis of parrhesia in classical (Greek and Roman) discourse holds special relevance for how we view AI's social role: as truth-teller, as performer, as stochastic parrot, as assistant, and so on.


I realise several of these texts also skate across traditions and language not all of you may be familiar with, and I'd encourage people supplement their readings with queries to AI itself. For example:

- Tell me about Austin's different kinds of speech acts, and how these relate to LLMs.
- How could Foucault's discussion of parrhesia relate to contemporary discussions about AI?

For those wanting a (slightly surreal) rendition of "The Drama Machine" paper rendered by AI, quite dramatically:

https://notebooklm.google.com/notebook/60047a4e-2674-4d6c-b896-67e5f3166580/audio

I'd of course encourage people who enjoy podcast enactments to explore other texts – as some of you have been doing.

  • Reading about attention mechanisms this week made me think about their role in language education. Wooldridge (2020) explains how early systems processed text word by word or leaned on strict rules. That approach made output sound stiff and often disconnected. Vaswani et al.’s (2017) transformer model changed this by allowing systems to consider whole passages and weigh relationships across words. That explains why current tools sound smoother and more context aware.

    For English language learners in higher education, this change matters a lot. A model that can hold onto context can move beyond correcting grammar. It can also give feedback on word choice, idiomatic phrasing, or overall tone across an entire essay. That is closer to the type of support a writing tutor offers than a grammar checker. I thought back to Weizenbaum’s ELIZA (1966). Even with its limits, people felt they were having a genuine exchange. Attention mechanisms make interactions feel even more like a dialogue, which could change how students approach writing practice.

    Still, Crawford (2021) reminds us that these conversations are never neutral. Models reflect the data they are trained on. Some voices get amplified while others are left out. For multilingual students already negotiating what “counts” as academic English, this matters. Cope and Kalantzis (2023) also argue that generative AI changes the way we think about pedagogy. That connects with Austin’s (1975) idea that language itself is action. A student prompt is not only a question, it is a performance that shapes the response they will receive.

    Looking ahead, I wonder how universities will use these tools in writing support. Attention-based models could make personalized feedback available to more students, including those who may not visit the writing center. But the risk is that students might take AI output at face value. The challenge will be teaching them to use AI as part of their process while still questioning its authority.

    References

    Austin, J. L. (1975). How to do things with words. Harvard University Press. https://archive.org/embed/HowToDoThingsWithWordsAUSTIN

  • This week’s readings were equal part fascinating and confounding. I am slowly making sense of the speech acts by Austin and Foucault’s concept of parrhesia. I have been leaning a lot more on GenAI to help me understand and contextualize each of these. Here’s what I have learned:
    Austin’s theory of speech acts (locutionary, illocutionary, and perlocutionary) provides a framework for understanding how language can do things. When applied to AI:
    • Locutionary Acts: AI systems like LLMs excel at producing grammatically correct and coherent text (e.g., generating a sentence like It's raining outside).
    • Illocutionary Acts: AI can mimic human intentions (e.g., saying I promise to help or I apologize), but it lacks genuine intentionality. The force of these acts depends on how users interpret them.
    • Perlocutionary Acts: AI can influence users’ emotions, decisions, or actions (e.g., persuading someone to buy a product or comforting someone with empathetic language). However, these effects are unintentional on the part of the AI.

    Foucault’s concept of parrhesia (frank or fearless speech) emphasizes the ethical responsibility of speaking truthfully, even at personal risk. AI systems are shaped by the power structures of their creators (e.g., corporations, governments). This raises questions about whose truths are being represented and whose voices are marginalized.
