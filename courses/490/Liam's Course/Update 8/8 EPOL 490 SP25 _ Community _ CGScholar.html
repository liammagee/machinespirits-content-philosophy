EPOL 490 SP25’s Updates

Cybernetic Societies - How do We Prepare People? And Machines? And People?

In this last and optional week, we attempt to distill our training – good machine learners that we are – into some pragmatic lessons and thoughts for what lies ahead. Freed temporarily from ever-watchful (though always loving) eyes of AI peer review, we can speculate as to the implications of AI for what we need to learn and need to teach.

We ended last week with a hypothetical series of questions:

Imagine you are teaching a class. You meet a new and unusual student, “Gen” (pronounced like "Jen" – apologies to any actual Jen’s!). “Gen” wants to be a teacher, but admits to having a strange problem: they say they are a synthetic being, and lack a sense of self. They say they worry this means their own students will see through them – not take them seriously.

Given this unique case of imposter syndrome – what can we do to help? How do we combine aspects of the history, technical underpinnings, pedagogical assumptions, alignment issues, critical perspectives and accelerationist debates to map out an education agenda for AI?

- What values do we want to align “Gen” to?
- What should happen when “Gen” needs to align aberrant human students? Does the human override the AI? Does it depend on the human? Does it depend on the values?
- How do we instill in this synthetic being the "metic" or "phronetic" – to use two Greek terms connoting practical wisdom – to navigate human communication situations?
- What curriculum, syllabus & rubric do we need?
- How would we assess & evaluate?
- How do we avoid gamification of evaluations – or is that integral to meta-cognition, true AI?

What, in short, would be our collective manifesto for AI in education?

And in response to all of this, we are also asking implicit questions about human learning. In the new pedagogical loops opened up by humans labelling and training machines that learn, who then in turn teach humans, we are faced with novel challenges of mutual imitation and differentiation. Do humans follow the machines? Or need to distinguish ourselves from them? And if so, how?

These questions – of how to teach AI, so that it can in turn help and teach us – stretch beyond the hypothetical. They drive corporate agendas for how to steer AI. We could argue these questions deserve being opened up to wider public discourse, and perhaps in certain ways – through open source, feedback loops and so on – they already are. As AI begins to play pivotal roles in public institutions, we might also ask whether these questions are now also integral to democratic governance.

 

Readings:
Łodzikowski, K., Foltz, P. W., & Behrens, J. T. (2024). Generative AI and Its Educational Implications. In T. Kourkoulou, A. O. Tzirides, B. Cope, & M. Kalantzis (Eds.), Trust and Inclusion in AI-Mediated Education: Where Human Learning Meets Learning Machines. Springer.

  • The Gen scenario made me think about how we would actually prepare a synthetic being to take on the role of teacher. If Gen worries about being “seen through,” that reminds me of the broader issue we’ve wrestled with all term: AI systems generate fluent, authoritative text but lack a real sense of understanding. In some ways, Gen’s imposter syndrome is the honest recognition of that gap.

    If I imagine designing a curriculum for Gen, it would need to combine technical training with what Kalantzis and Cope call cyber-social learning (Łodzikowski et al., 2024). That means teaching Gen to work in feedback loops with humans, always leaving final judgment with people. But more than that, Gen would need a grounding in education justice — understanding that its role is not just to deliver content but to amplify equity and help students use their authentic voices.

    This is where the connections to the larger literature matter. Crawford’s Atlas of AI (2021) reminds us that these systems are not abstract: they are built on resource extraction, labor exploitation, and institutional choices. A manifesto for teaching Gen would have to include transparency about those origins. And “A Brief History of AI” shows how the field has always been shaped by behaviorist and cybernetic traditions.
    When we leave out the history of AI, we end up circling back to the same problems—treating alignment mainly as control instead of as a chance to foster growth and dialogue. If I had to boil down my own view into a single line, I’d say I want an AI that supports human learning without taking over human judgment, that admits its own limits, and that helps classrooms move toward equity.

    References

    Crawford, K. (2021). Atlas of AI: Power, politics, and the planetary costs of artificial intelligence. Yale University Press.

    Łodzikowski, K., Foltz, P. W., & Behrens, J. T. (2024). Generative AI and its educational implications. In T. Kourkoulou, A. O. Tzirides, B. Cope, & M. Kalantzis (Eds.), Trust and inclusion in AI-mediated education: Where human learning meets learning machines. Springer.

    Russell, S., & Norvig, P. (2021). Artificial intelligence: A modern approach (4th ed.). Pearson. [for “A Brief History of AI” overview]

  • The convergence of human and synthetic intelligence raises profound questions about education that we've rarely confronted before. Professor, your Gen thought experiment brilliantly illustrates the recursive nature of teaching and learning in an AI-integrated future. As a computer science student with experience in traditional programming paradigms, I find myself wondering how our current rigid computational approaches might evolve to accommodate the phronetic wisdom you reference.
    Your questions about alignment touch on a central paradox: how can we instill values in synthetic beings when human values themselves remain contested? Perhaps the answer lies not in perfecting alignment but in creating transparent frameworks where both humans and machines recognize their limitations and biases.
    The curriculum for AI education must balance technical rigor with philosophical inquiry. I'm particularly struck by your observation about novel challenges of mutual imitation and differentiation. This suggests a future where education isn't simply humans teaching machines or vice versa, but a collaborative endeavor where distinctions between teacher and student blur.
    Your call for wider public discourse resonates deeply. As AI increasingly shapes our institutions, democratic governance of these technologies becomes not just desirable but essential for preserving human agency in cybernetic societies.

  • The questions asked this week would be much more difficult to answer without the support of our weekly readings and discussions in this course. I'll do my best to home in on the central theme of what I see as the relationship between humans and machines in a case where a machine might be considered for a teaching position (in light of our previous readings).

    Skimming through chapters of ''Trust and Inclusion in AI-Mediated Education: Where Human Learning Meets Learning Machines my interest gravitated to Chapter 1 authored by our LDL professors Kalantzis and Cope, On Cyber-Social Learning — A Critique of Artificial Intelligence in Education. Since our class has been discussing manifestos at great length within the past 2 weeks, I was drawn to the manifesto (p. 27) in this reading, which the authors introduce as an agenda for action for their conclusions regarding human and machine.

    I'm particularly drawn to Step #7 which states to: ''Develop a Program of Education Justice!'' (p. 29) which I interpret as proposing to apply principles of cyber-social learning where a possibility of balance can be achieved with humans and machines collaborating within a feedback loop. As humans and machines have a different kinds of capacity, they complement one another instead of competing and as such they can create a balance between the two extremes of opinion--the AI accelerationists and the AI doomsayers.

    As part of the concept of Cyber-Social Learning, there is the understanding that instead of approaching AI as a replicated human intelligence, it is viewed as an alternative type of capacity that is different from human intelligence, yet, which can help humans achieve tasks in learning, teaching and knowledge accumulation through a combined process of back and forth communication, yet allowing the human to perform final review and final decision-making of the collaborative process and final results.

    So to me it is important to distinguish the difference between human and machine, with the human teacher supervising classes and allowing Gen to assist as a specialized TA with very specific duties and limitations. I would communicate to Gen my thoughts about this situation, but explain that Gen's help would be greatly appreciated as long as they followed their supervising teacher's directives.

    [Note: I am not considering the fact that it is extremely unlikely or impossible for a ''synthetic'' applicant to ever apply or be considered for a teaching position for so many school institutions' regulating reasons/modes of identification/background checks outside of the theories we have read about and discussed].
