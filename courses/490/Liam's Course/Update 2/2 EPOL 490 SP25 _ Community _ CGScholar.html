EPOL 490 SP25’s Updates

How does a Machine Learn?

All of you should have received a work request on Monday which is essentially the main project, you'll be working on throughout this term. As you work through your blogposts and refine your main project, allow your topic to evolve - it could become more specific, broader, or even change completely. Remember, the goal is to gain new insights and see your topic in a different light by the end of the course. Here are the key timelines to keep in mind regarding your main project:

  • AI review & draft submission for peer review: February 16
  • Peer review completion: March 2
  • Revisions & submission for TA/Instructor review: March 9
  • Final publication: March 10

More detailed instructions regarding these timelines and the process involved will be shared either through the newsletter or admin updates in the coming weeks. Keep an eye out for those!

This week we will look at getting some general intuition into the complex topic of machine learning. A number of readings are assigned to this week, all quite technical (and many pivotal in the development of AI). My strong recommendation will be to watch the Grant Sanderson video – a master class in exposition from an expert in conveying visual intuitions around the mathematics of AI – and then download and scan or skim-read as many of these papers as possible. I would spend a little time on (a) Markov, (b) Mikolov et al., and (c) Vaswani et al, as each of these papers marks a critical conceptual as well as technical transition in the history of generative AI.

For people without technical backgrounds, do not worry if none of this makes any sense! While a proper understanding of deep learning would require many courses, we will be breaking things down in a way that hopefully provides some intuition in class.

  • This week’s materials helped me connect some of the history of machine learning with the systems we are using now. Watching Sanderson (2024) was especially helpful. The way he walked through tokenization and attention made it easier to see why transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) became such a turning point. Instead of processing language word by word, attention mechanisms let models take in the bigger picture all at once. That shift explains why today’s large language models feel so much more responsive and fluent than earlier approaches.

    The Markov (2006) reading reminded me that statistical modeling of text has been around for more than a century. Even then, researchers were asking how the structure of language could be captured through probability. Mikolov et al. (2013) showed the next leap when they introduced word embeddings, allowing words to be represented by their relationships in vector space. Put together, these pieces show a clear path from early statistical chains to today’s generative AI.

    As someone working in language education, I was struck by how these same mechanisms could reshape feedback and assessment. If a model can weigh context through attention, then it might be able to give students not only grammatical corrections but also feedback that captures nuance and usage. At the same time, Shannon’s (1948) work on information theory reminds us that communication is always shaped by choices about what counts as signal and what counts as noise. That feels like a caution for education: what patterns are we training models to see, and what are they overlooking?

    I’m left thinking about how attention-based models might open up new ways to individualize learning, but also how important it will be to question the assumptions built into the data they are trained on.

    Markov, A. A. (2006). An example of statistical investigation of the text Eugene Onegin concerning the connection of samples in chains. Science in Context, 19(4), 591–600. https://alpha60.de/research/markov/DavidLink_AnExampleOfStatistical_MarkovTrans_2007.pdf

  • When I think about transformers, my mind immediately goes to predictive text and autocorrect on our cell phones and computers. It seems like these were some of the first real AI tools we interacted with daily, but, after our readings, I know that AI actually goes back much further.

    From our readings and some digging, I learned that the field of artificial intelligence started in the 1950s with early rule-based systems and concepts like the Turing Test. Over the decades, AI evolved from expert systems to machine learning, gaining momentum in the 1990s and early 2000s. Predictive text, like T9 and early autocorrect, was one of the first widely used consumer-facing AI applications, using statistical models to predict words based on common patterns. While those early systems were simple compared to today's AI, they marked a shift from rigid rules to adaptive learning. The real breakthrough came with deep learning and transformers in the 2010s, leading to the powerful language models and virtual assistants we rely on now. Even though predictive text wasn’t the first generation of AI, it introduced many of us to the idea of machines that could anticipate our needs, laying the foundation for the AI-driven world we live in today.

  • Reflecting on the Week 2 readings as well as the activity we did in class last week, I feel a lot more grounded in the foundations of machine learning and deep learning in particular. Though our class experiment in character pairings of poetry lines only resulted in Lorem Ipsum-like text outputs by the model, the instance where Michele and I both used a poem containing the word “beneath” had me thinking about the data that is in a particular model’s training set. Beneath is not necessarily a common connecting word like ‘and’ or ‘the,’ but I would expect that the model would put together words with the pairings present in beneath moreso than those with pairings that exist in other words that were not present in multiple poem lines, just from probability standpoint with dot products. If this were to be true—and presumably is true in much larger language models—the question of what representative data should be used by a particular model that is to be used in various (or specific) contexts comes to mind.

    Through my group’s work on an AI assistant over the past 20 months or so, there has been a shift—specifically, from focusing on an AI engine based on a “ground truths” corpus and a vector database of web pages to engines based on pulling from live web results—for various reasons (things like the required frequency of scraping and the fine-tuning required). This week, however, I am starting to gain more of an understanding of the technical structures and functions that undergird these directions (and hopefully that understanding is correct… or more correct!). For instance, the Sanderson video helped me to visually understand the concept of a vector (or set of numbers that represent a point or a direction in a multidimensional space) that is specific to a token, and how those vectors can be read by a machine and related to other vectors in attention blocks and multilayer perceptrons. Also, this particular quote at about the 8:35 mark was particularly insightful in differentiating the two blocks that were introduced (and are repeated in a deep learning model): “Insofar as prediction requires context, this is where the attention blocks are relevant, and insofar as prediction requires just general knowledge from the world, these perceptrons give extra capacity to store some more of that” (Sanderson, 2024).

    Looking deeper into that attention block through Attention Is All You Need (Vaswani et al., 2017 [2023]), it was fascinating to learn a bit more about the network architecture of the Transformer. “Self-attention, sometimes called intra-attention is an attention mechanism relating different positions
    of a single sequence in order to compute a representation of the sequence….To the best of our knowledge, however, the Transformer is the first transduction model relying entirely on self-attention to compute representations of its input and output without using sequence aligned RNNs or convolution” (p. 8). My reading was that the self-attention quality of the Transformer architecture allows for much faster training of the model.

    Though I am really experiencing the “tip of the iceberg” feeling of uncovering some of these technical building blocks like matrix multiplications, it is good to know a little bit more about many of the concepts I hear about at work (that I don’t stop and ask the developers to explain-ha!) or in the news. In many ways, I am cautious about my own contributions to the discussion of AI in my field because I want to have a much stronger foundation in the pathways to AI before making an offering. Full disclosure: my level of caution grew after taking in McCarthy’s review of The Question of Artificial Intelligence last week (McCarthy, 2000), and his criticisms of S.G. Shankar’s and J. Schropman’s chapters in particular; I could hear a mini John McCarthy on my shoulder saying “not so fast, Ch’nel, you need to get the foundational events and principles right before building on any of this!” Still scratching the surface, but I’ve started the journey, at least.

  • This week's readings and viewings, particularly Visualizing Transformers and Attention, were really interesting to me. As an English teacher who has also taught English as a Secondary Language (ESL) and as a Foreign Language (EFL), the logic behind generating meaning through word and sentence structure has always been fascinating to me. If a foreign language student says a sentence that is grammatically correct but unidiomatic, it sounds off to us as native English speakers. Why? Because we have an established pattern by which we typically express that particular thought, and to express it a different way sounds weird. A foreign language learner doesn't have that context - yet - and so the only rules they are playing by are mechanical. When I taught English in Korea, idioms were the running joke: No, one student would correct another, that sounds funny to Mr. Jones, so you have to say it like this.

    It really feels like LLMs, particularly generative AI, are using the same sort of logic. They aren't just asking themselves what would be grammatically correct, because that alone isn't enough to determine correctness when it comes to producing and ordering words. There are contexts at play, and they seem to be using the frequency of one use of language over another as the mechanism by which to determine those contexts. It's like my students asking, Is that how Mr. Jones would say it? but in a much larger scale of, Is this how the sources I have been trained on would respond to this prompt? And that's really cool! My work in this course is using AI tools in online writing instruction, and I feel like understanding this model and logic system is helpful in determining how an LLM could be used as a writing tool because of that logic system. I always advised my EFL students to watch English-speaking television and videos because exposure creates familiarity which helps in understanding, and I feel like an AI tool could be a great resource to help with just that.
