Common Pitfalls of Literature Reviews

  1. Length - some reviews extend to 15k, 25k, 30k words. This is too long!
  2. Conversely, no signposting - straight into “X said this, Y said that”
    • Issue of the “so what” question. Why does the reader care, or need to know this?
    • Having sympathy for the reader / reviewer / examiner…
  3. “Gap” not justified by the preceding review. The review must read like a (legal?) dossier of evidence about the claim of gaps
    • Styles may differ (e.g. Spivak (1988) and Zhang et al. (2025))

  2. Structure and metadiscourse - guide your reader through the evidence (AI as roleplay - lawyer?)

If it helps…

  • A literature review concludes with an argument for existence of gaps
  • This argument must be proved: corroborated by the evidence supplied in the review
  • This is hard to do and difficult to learn!

Structuring Your Literature Review

Pick a principle: - General > Specific – can confirm but also contrast (“General theories of pedagogy suggest… However studies of adult language learners also show…”) - Chronological (“By the 1960s, discussions of AI had already become more muted…”) - Thematic (“The concept of ‘attention’ is used in roughly similar ways in machine and human learning…”) - Spatial (“Studies in Asian universities show that …; in contrast, findings from North American universities suggest…”) - Developmental (“First I will present an outline of the mind/body problem. I then move on to look at critiques of the very distinction itself…”) - Other models? - Nested Categories (chronology then thematic, etc)?

Some Guiding Notes

  • Specialization: Can work well when you want to convey a sense of narrowing down a focus: Education > K12 > Math learning > Algebra > in rural Mid-west schools
  • Chronological: Can work well when you want to convey a sense of progress
    • And how your work extends that progress
  • Thematic: Can work well when you want to convey a sense of overlaps, intersection and gaps
    • And how your work discovers and fills one of these gaps

Some Guiding Notes (2)

  • Spatial: Works well when you want to convey a sense of cultural, spatial or geographic difference
    • And how your study attends to a location or zone not previously studied
  • Developmental: Works well when you plan to build your argument through a sequence of positions / thinkers / ideas
    • And show how you will blend, contrast or critique all of these positions / thinkers / ideas

Nested categories

  • 3 themes - each with their own history
  • Or: General > Special - special might involve chronological and spatial differences. “General pedagogy theories developed in the early/mid-twentieth century of pedagogy suggest… More recent studies suggest national and local cultures play a greater role. For instance, Indian scholars of K12 learning have shown…”

How do I Decide?

  • Often iterative:
    • Go forward to discover the gap
    • Move back from the gap to discover the structure
  • Experiment with different structures
    • Mock up your structure (more on this soon)

Metadiscourse / Signposting

  • Assume your reader does not know the literature
  • A literature review is often pedagogical - teaching the reader about a field, in order to persuade them about the gap your research fills
  • Helps you in authoring - does Y really follow from X?
  • Be prepared to write / re-write this metadiscursive text often - especially early, as you experiment with structural principles (chronological etc)

Making It Explicit

  • Cannot be too explicit about your structure:
    • Introduction (future tense):
      • “In this literature review I will…”
      • (or if you prefer) “This review will…”
    • Conclusion (past tense):
      • “In this review, I have discussed literature relating to…”
      • (or if you prefer) “This review has…”
    • Along the way:
      • “In the last section, I discussed…. In the next, I will…”
      • Can be blended through connective language. “While the preceding studies illustrated how use of AI in education has been studied extensively, less has been said about how AI contributes to the student/teacher relationship. Some exceptions include studies by X, Y and Z…”

Sociology of Knowledge

  • Study of academic / scholarly societies
  • Navel gazing? Perhaps. But relevant for all academia because it helps us calibrate our writing (tone / citations )
  • We’ve already touched on this
    • Two Cultures (Snow) – Humanities and the Sciences)
  • But also: what are financial / political drivers of research? What are the conceptual or ideological frames? What are the standards for evidence or truth? Compare Spivak (1988) to Zhang et al. (2025)

Sociology of Knowledge: Key figures

  • Karl Marx (19th century): How do material conditions influence thought?
  • Emile Durkheim (1912): The Elementary Forms of Religious Life
  • Sigmund Freud (1930): Civilization and its Discontents
  • Karl Mannheim (1929): Ideology and Utopia
  • Thomas Kuhn (1962): The Structure of Scientific Revolutions
  • Berger, Luckmann (1966): The Social Construction of Reality
  • Bourdieu (1977): Outline of a Theory of Practice
  • Michel Foucault (1966, 1969): The Order of Things, The Archaeology of Knowledge
  • David Bloor (1976), Knowledge and social imagery
  • Bruno Latour, Steve Woolgar (1979), Laboratory Life
  • Edward Said (1976), Orientalism
  • Gayatri Spivak (1987), In Other Words
  • Judith Butler (1990), Gender Trouble

Sociology of Knowledge: Common Points?

  • Thinking is not independent of conditions of life
  • What are “conditions”?
    • Class (Marx), race, gender (Said, Butler, Spivak)
    • Social facts - fads & fashions (Durkheim)
    • Ideology - what fundamental beliefs structure or make possible our thinking (Mannheim)
    • Paradigms (Kuhn), epistemes (Foucault), habitus (Bourdieu): background forms of knowing and being
  • Related to unconscious bias (Freud)

Why does this matter?

  • Often asked to account for your positionality
  • Hard to do! What is your unconscious bias? What are you conscious of being unconscious of?
    • If you knew, it wouldn’t be unconscious!
  • Irregular verbs:
    • “I think critically and independently”
    • “You think according to the influences of your economic class and upbringing”
    • “She / He / They think as unaware and naive victims of ideology”

Questions about an academic community:

  • What is its jargon? Do you want to reproduce, challenge or modify this jargon?
  • What are its standards of evidence? What does it find persuasive? What are the key questions it is asking?
  • How stringent are its expectations about method, citation, formatting?
  • What are its markers of distinction and discernment – “tickets to the game”?
  • Conversely: what seem to be the things not to say?

How can you develop this tacit sense? Options:

  • Treat it like a research project - study academics like an community
    • Read journals
    • Attend conferences
    • Observe faculty
    • Join committees
    • Read or scan ‘campus’ novels & drama
    • Maintain a reflective journal
    • Share your own research, ask for feedback
    • Ask AI to play the role of an academic in a field

Relationship to Literature Review

  • Write with your audience in mind
  • But - very important! – not as though you are already “in the club”
  • Take a reference to Spivak. You might know your audience is likely familiar with Spivak’s work. But of course you don’t say:
    • “As we all know, Spivak argued that”
    • Instead it is as though your audience forgot Spivak’s point and you gently remind them: “Spivak argued that…”
    • But then you might twist this familiarity: “In the age of AI, it might seem Spivak’s arguments regarding grammatization have dated. However,…”
  • So a literature review can translate your own sociological research into some careful phrasing that both reassures and surprises your community
