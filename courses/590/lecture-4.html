Slow Reading with AI: Spivak, Can the Subaltern Speak?

  • Why? Complex example of a humanities text. Presumes knowledge of Western philosophy, political debates around representation
  • “Classic” contribution to decolonial, feminist debates. But long, hard to parse / summarize
    • Spivak often criticized for performative contradiction:
      • Argues for universalization of education, including the most excluded (the rural, illiterate, women) - the “subaltern”
      • But references US academia, French & German philosophy, English colonization, old & new Indian scholarship… who can understand her?
  • So… good case study for AI
  • Use the Google Doc to make collective notes as we go
    • Cyber-social Reading? ‘Vibe Scholarship’ in action?

Group Reading with AI: Some questions (but develop your own)

  • What is “subaltern”? Why this term?
  • What is Spivak doing exactly with her literature?
    • Contra Foucault and Deleuze? Why? And why use Marx against them?
    • Who else does Spivak assemble in what we might call the “drama” that she stages?
    • Why no (or few) US scholars? Who are the key French and German figures in her analysis?
    • Who are the Indian scholars, and how are these voices mobilized?
  • What is the significance of the two German translations of representation: Vorstellung and Darstellung?

Structuring Your Literature Review

Pick a principle: - General > Specific – can confirm but also contrast (“General theories of pedagogy suggest… However studies of adult language learners also show…”) - Chronological (“By the 1960s, discussions of AI had already become more muted…”) - Thematic (“The concept of ‘attention’ is used in roughly similar ways in machine and human learning…”) - Spatial (“Studies in Asian universities show that …; in contrast, findings from North American universities suggest…”) - Developmental (“First I will discuss Descartes’ presentation of the mind/body problem. I then move on to look at 20th century critiques of this distinction….”) - Other? - Nested Categories (chronology then thematic, etc)?

Some Guiding Notes

  • Specialization: Can work well when you want to convey a sense of narrowing down a focus: Education > K12 > Math learning > Algebra > in rural Mid-west schools
  • Chronological: Can work well when you want to convey a sense of progress
    • And how your work extends that progress
  • Thematic: Can work well when you want to convey a sense of overlaps, intersection and gaps
    • And how your work discovers and fills one of these gaps
  • Spatial: Works well when you want to convey a sense of cultural, spatial or geographic difference
    • And how your study attends to a location or zone not previously studied
  • Developmental: Works well when you plan to build your argument through a sequence of positions / thinkers / ideas
    • And show how you will blend, contrast or critique all of these positions / thinkers / ideas

How do I Decide?

  • Typically iterative:
    • Go forward to discover the gap
    • Move back from the gap to discover the structure
  • Experiment with different structures
    • Mock up your structure (more on this next week)

Metadiscourse / Signposting

  • Assume your reader does not know the literature
  • A literature review is often pedagogical - teaching the reader about a field, in order to persuade them about the gap your research fills
  • Helps you in authoring - does Y really follow from X?
  • Be prepared to write / re-write this metadiscursive text often - especially early, as you experiment with structural principles (chronological etc)

Making It Explicit

  • Cannot be too explicit about your structure:
    • Introduction (future tense):
      • “In this literature review I will…”
      • (or if you prefer) “This review will…”
    • Conclusion (past tense):
      • “In this review, I have discussed literature relating to…”
      • (or if you prefer) “This review has…”
    • Along the way:
      • “In the last section, I discussed…. In the next, I will…”
      • Can be blended through connective language. “While the preceding studies illustrated how use of AI in education has been studied extensively, less has been said about how AI contributes to the student/teacher relationship. Some exceptions include studies by X, Y and Z…”
